Prior to the advent of large-scale genetic studies of complex traits, WFS1 was primarily implicated in Wolfram syndrome, a rare recessive disorder caused by any of some 200 mutations in the gene, including indels and missense variants. (A milder form of the disease, Wolfram-like syndrome, is caused by different mutations in WFS1.) Patients with Wolfram syndrome typically develop type 2 diabetes after the endoplasmic reticulum in β cells fails.

In 2007, a study of 1,536 SNPs in 84 genes regulating β cell development, growth, function and survival found that several SNPs in or near WFS1 were significantly associated with type 2 diabetes in populations of European ancestry. (All SNPs were in LD with the intronic SNP rs10010131, the strongest hit.) The following year, a genotyping study of 16 SNPs in 16,061 Swedish and 2,770 Finnish research participants also found an association between type 2 diabetes and rs10010131 (OR = 1.12; P = 0.001).

Subsequent genome-wide association studies of type 2 diabetes have implicated two other variants in WFS1: rs4689388 (OR = 1.16, P = 1x10_8) and rs1801214 (OR = 1.13, P = 3x10-8). Both variants reach genome-wide significance in exome chip data included in this portal.
